Tea Corner

A running list of what’s in my current rotation & where I get it.

Current Rotation

  • Da Yu Lin Spring

    Taiwan · High Mountain Oolong

    Red Blossom Tea Company

    From one of Taiwan’s highest elevation growing regions at ~2,600m. The spring harvest produces a clean, crisp oolong with floral lift and a lingering finish.

    85–90°C · 2–3 min

  • Kenya Milima

    Kenya · Single Estate Black

    Harney & Sons

    A bright, high-grown Kenyan black from the Milima estate. Brisk and clean with a distinctive berry-like note that sets it apart from most breakfast teas.

    95–100°C · 3–4 min

  • Honey Black Tea

    Taiwan · Mi Xiang Hong Cha 🍯

    Chafolio

    Naturally sweet and aromatic because the leaves are bitten by leafhoppers before harvest, triggering the plant’s defense response. The result is an unmistakably honey-like character with no added anything.

    90–95°C · 2–3 min

  • Imperial Shou Pu‑erh 2012

    Yunnan, China · Aged Ripe Pu‑erh

    Red Blossom Tea Company

    Over a decade of aging on a ripe pu‑erh rounds out the earthiness into something smooth and almost chocolatey. One of the more forgiving teas to brew — hard to overbrew or burn it.

    95–100°C · 3–5 min

  • Lost Maples

    Texas, USA · Yaupon Holly

    CatSpring Yaupon

    Yaupon (Ilex vomitoria) is the only caffeinated plant native to North America, and Lost Maples is a standout. Clean, smooth, and surprisingly tea-like — none of the grassiness you might expect.

    90–95°C · 3–4 min

  • Red Tea Resin

    Yunnan, China · Compressed Red Tea

    Jesse’s Tea House

    A concentrated pressed-resin format that dissolves directly in hot water — no leaves to deal with. Rich, full-bodied, and surprisingly nuanced for how convenient it is.

    95–100°C · dissolve 1 piece per cup

Where I Get It

  • Red Blossom Tea Company

    831 Grant Ave, San Francisco, CA 94108

    Source for the Da Yu Lin Spring and the 2012 Shou Pu‑erh. SF Chinatown shop with a very deep catalog of Taiwanese and Chinese teas.

  • Harney & Sons

    Online

    Source for the Kenya Milima. Wide range of single-estate and blended teas, consistently well-sourced.

  • Chafolio

    Online

    Source for the Honey Black Tea. Specializes in small-farm teas with good sourcing transparency.

  • CatSpring Yaupon

    CatSpring, Texas

    Source for Lost Maples. Grows and processes yaupon holly in the Texas Hill Country — one of the few producers making a serious case for America’s native caffeinated plant.

  • Jesse’s Tea House

    Online

    Source for the Red Tea Resin. Specialty shop focused on pressed and aged teas from Yunnan.
